Full social media strategy

Executive snapshot

Monte Carlo Technologies is a digital solutions partner: custom websites, SEO, marketing, and business automation (including AI agents). Social’s job is discovery (portfolio + proof), trust (process, transparency, founder voice), and pipeline (discovery calls, audits, project inquiries) for U.S./English-speaking SMBs that compare agencies heavily before they buy.

Brand positioning

One-liner: High-performing websites, measurable SEO, and automation — built for SMBs that want fewer vendors and clearer outcomes.

Onlyness (vs alternatives):

  • Vs template / DIY: Custom craft, performance, and integration — not “another theme.”
  • Vs single-service shops: Design, build, growth, and automation under one coordinated team.
  • Vs enterprise consultancies: Speed, accessibility, and pricing suited to growing SMBs — without corporate bloat.

Voice & tone: Clear, confident, ROI-minded; explain technical topics in plain English; show work and process.

Audience targeting (organic + paid)

  • Geography: Primarily U.S./Canada English-speaking markets; global English acceptable for remote positioning.
  • Segments: Founders, marketing managers, ops/sales leaders at SMBs (often 1–100 employees); professional services, local services, light e‑commerce, tech startups.
  • Intent stacks: Website redesign, SEO agency, custom software, landing page, AI automation, “WordPress vs custom,” etc.
  • Creative split: Rotate service education vs proof/credibility vs personal brand — matching your export Category taxonomy.

Dual daily feed rhythm (Reach + Prime)

Organic social is scheduled as two feed touchpoints per day on the primary mirror (Facebook + Instagram), with default ET slots documented in the editor calendar:

  • Slot A — Reach: Algorithm-forward hooks (short Reels, polls, comment CTAs). Use the same Category and Topic/Angle when the Reach post tees up the evening idea so exports stay comparable week to week.
  • Slot B — Prime: Deeper education, demo, or proof — typically a clearer consult / audit or site CTA.
  • Goals column: Reach rows aim at discovery; Prime rows skew Leads (offer-heavy) or Trust (brand, testimony, culture) — see calendar Goal field.
  • Stories: Optional; not counted in the 60 feed rows. Use for polls, stickers, and resharing strong Reach clips.

Funnel and monetization

  1. Awareness: Reels, carousels, hot takes, SEO/UX micro-lessons.
  2. Consideration: Case patterns, process explainers, comparisons (custom vs template, SEO timelines).
  3. Conversion: Discovery call / consultation / audit request — align CTA wording with live site offers.
  4. Offer stack: Projects (web, landing, branding), retainers (SEO, growth), automation/AI builds — frame each post to one primary next step.

What weekly metrics imply — actions

Exports show many posts with very low reach and occasional zero engagement, alongside stronger performance on select Reels and topic mixes (e.g. Service Solution, Educational, Personal Branding). Typical implications:

  • Distribution: Put budget behind best hooks — especially Reach-slot winners once you have engagement signal; repurpose winning Reels across platforms.
  • Creative: Batch short-form with clear hooks; A/B first 2 seconds; add on-screen text for sound-off.
  • Timing: Validate posting windows against Meta “When your followers are online” and your Timing & Frequency.html Best Time Slot rows (tentative).
  • Offer clarity: Weak CTAs correlate with scroll-past; pair educational posts with one explicit next step.

Audience psychology breakdown

Grounded in SMB buyers comparing digital partners: time-poor, skeptical after bad vendor experiences, and hungry for proof before a big spend.

  • Fragmented vendors (designer + dev + SEO + freelancer) with no single owner of outcomes.
  • Sites that look fine but don’t rank, load slowly, or fail on mobile.
  • Opaque timelines, surprise scope creep, or invoices that don’t map to deliverables.
  • Past SEO/marketing disappointments — “we paid for months and saw nothing.”
  • Manual workflows and tool sprawl; curiosity about AI but fear of hype.

  • Credibility: A site and social presence that matches how good they are in real life.
  • Predictability: Clear phases, milestones, and reporting — especially for SEO and builds.
  • Leverage: Automation and systems that save hours without hiring another full-time role.
  • One partner: Fewer meetings, fewer handoffs — design, build, and growth aligned.

  • Choosing the wrong agency and losing months of runway.
  • Overpaying for a pretty site that doesn’t convert.
  • Automation/AI that breaks workflows or alienates customers.
  • Being sold a roadmap they can’t maintain internally.

  • Google search, Clutch-style directories, LinkedIn, and peer referrals.
  • Portfolio skim on IG/website within seconds — visual quality is a filter.
  • “DM for details” or form fills only after trust markers (case, process, reviews).

Authority pillars (personal brand)

  1. Operator clarity: Explain trade-offs (scope, timeline, stack) without condescension.
  2. Show the work: Short screen captures, before/after metrics (with client permission), design breakdowns.
  3. Proof stack: Testimonials, logos, case patterns, “how we rescued a stalled project.”
  4. Community & hiring: Internships, culture, partnerships — signal stability.
  5. Support narrative: What happens after launch — maintenance, SEO cadence, iteration.

Content pillars that convert

Each pillar maps to project-scope service lines and to the 60-post editor calendar (Reach + Prime slots). Use Category + Topic/Angle from your weekly exports for reporting consistency.

Pillar 1 — Credibility & process

Objective: Reduce buyer anxiety; show how you work.

Example posts

  • “Our 4 phases from kickoff to launch (what happens every week).”
  • Carousel: FAQs on timelines, revisions, and ownership.
  • Video: “Red flags we see in RFPs — and how we fix them.”

Category lean: Company Branding · Personal Branding · Angles: About Us, Educational.

Pillar 2 — Web, UX & product craft

Objective: Prove design/dev quality; teach conversion basics.

Example posts

  • Reel: hero section teardown (above-the-fold clarity).
  • “Custom vs template — when each makes sense.”
  • Case stills: mobile performance, accessibility callouts.

Services: Custom Web, UI/UX, Landing pages · Angles: Service Solution, Educational.

Pillar 3 — SEO & measurable growth

Objective: Build trust with ROI language; demystify SEO.

Example posts

  • “What belongs in a technical SEO audit (plain English).”
  • Graph pattern: baseline → 90 days (illustrative, anonymized).
  • Thread: local vs national SEO for SMBs.

Service: SEO · Angles: Educational, Problem/Solution, Facts.

Pillar 4 — Software, PWA & platforms

Objective: Show depth beyond marketing sites — integrations, scale.

Example posts

  • “When a business outgrows off-the-shelf tools.”
  • PWA explainer: offline, install, speed — SMB use cases.
  • Architecture diagram teaser (sanitized).

Services: Custom Software, PWA · Angles: Service Solution, Educational.

Pillar 5 — Brand & graphic systems

Objective: Win visual-first buyers; show cohesion across channels.

Example posts

  • Grid: identity system applied to web + social + print.
  • “Three signs your brand looks ‘DIY’ on ads.”
  • Motion sting / logo animation loop.

Service: Graphic Design · Angles: Educational, Behind The Scenes.

Pillar 6 — AI agents, automation & proof

Objective: De-risk AI hype; tie to time saved and better CX; stack social proof.

Example posts

  • “Automation stack: form → CRM → slack alert (demo).”
  • “Human handoff rules we ship with every agent.”
  • Testimonial carousel / quote cards (permissioned).

Service: AI Agent & automation · Angles: Problem/Solution, Service Solution, Testimony.
